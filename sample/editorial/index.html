I. The E-Gallon

What the outlet would charge, if it pumped.

An e-gallon is the cost of driving the same distance a gallon of gasoline covers — priced in kilowatt-hours at your local utility. In no state does it come out ahead for gas.

The spread is narrowest in Hawai'i, widest in Washington. Everywhere else it is a steady, unremarkable fact of modern arithmetic: electrons are cheaper than combustion.

On the maintenance of moving parts.

An internal combustion engine has roughly two thousand moving parts. A battery has one. Reports of the EV's fragility have been, in the phrase, considerably exaggerated.

Consider the spark plug, which you do not in fact consider, because you have never replaced one and do not intend to. The plug, its gap, its threading, the four to eight of them in your engine bay — they are there because gasoline will not combust on its own. Electricity will.

Over five years, the median American driver spends $3,410 maintaining a gas car: oil changes, air filters, spark plugs, belts, coolant flushes, transmission services, exhaust repairs, and the steady tax of parts that wear because they move.

The same driver spends $1,570 maintaining an EV. Tires, brake fluid, cabin filter, wiper blades. The difference — $1,840 — is the invisible dividend of mechanical simplicity.

This has the odd consequence of making EVs less satisfying to their owners' mechanics, who now see them only when something goes dramatically wrong, which is almost never.

The mechanic's loss is the driver's gain. A car with fewer parts has fewer opportunities to fail, and fewer bills to mail. For families who keep a vehicle past warranty, the maintenance delta alone can equal a year of gasoline.

There is, of course, the battery. Manufacturers guarantee the pack for eight years or a hundred thousand miles. In practice, packs built after 2020 are projected to deliver eighty percent of original capacity at the fifteen-year mark — longer than most gas engines go without a major service.
